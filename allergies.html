Allergies & Anaphylactic Shock

Life-threatening emergency treatment

üö® Signs of Anaphylactic Shock (Call 911 IMMEDIATELY):

  • Breathing problems - Wheezing, difficulty breathing, tight throat
  • Swelling - Face, lips, tongue, throat, or other body parts
  • Skin reactions - Widespread hives, severe itching, flushed or pale skin
  • Circulatory problems - Rapid/weak pulse, dizziness, fainting
  • Digestive issues - Nausea, vomiting, severe cramping, diarrhea
  • Sense of doom - Feeling that something terrible is happening

Emergency Treatment for Anaphylaxis:

  1. Call 911 immediately - Even if symptoms seem mild at first
  2. Use EpiPen if available - Inject into outer thigh (see instructions below)
  3. Have person lie down - Elevate legs unless they're having breathing trouble
  4. Loosen tight clothing - Help them breathe easier
  5. Cover with blanket - Treat for shock
  6. Monitor breathing and pulse - Be ready to perform CPR
  7. Don't give oral medications - If person is vomiting or having trouble swallowing
  8. Prepare for second injection - Symptoms may return in 10-20 minutes

How to Use an EpiPen:

  1. Remove safety cap - Pull off blue safety release
  2. Hold firmly - Grip with dominant hand, orange tip pointing down
  3. Position on outer thigh - Can be used through clothing
  4. Swing and push firmly - Push orange tip against thigh until you hear a click
  5. Hold for 3 seconds - Keep in place for full medication delivery
  6. Remove and massage - Massage injection site for 10 seconds
  7. Call 911 if not already done - Even if symptoms improve
  8. Keep used EpiPen - Show to emergency responders

For Mild Allergic Reactions:

  • Remove or avoid allergen - If known and possible
  • Take antihistamine - Benadryl or similar (follow package directions)
  • Apply cool compress - For localized swelling or hives
  • Monitor closely - Watch for worsening symptoms
  • Seek medical attention - If symptoms persist or worsen

‚ö†Ô∏è Important EpiPen Facts:

  • EpiPen effects last only 10-20 minutes
  • Always call 911 even after using EpiPen
  • Keep EpiPens at room temperature (not in car or freezer)
  • Check expiration dates regularly
  • Carry two EpiPens if prescribed (second dose may be needed)
  • Tell friends, family, and coworkers where EpiPen is kept

Common Severe Allergens:

  • Foods - Peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, eggs, milk, soy, wheat
  • Medications - Penicillin, aspirin, certain antibiotics
  • Insect stings - Bees, wasps, hornets, fire ants
  • Environmental - Latex, certain plants (poison ivy severe cases)
  • Other - Exercise-induced, cold temperatures, certain dyes

‚ùå Don't Do This During Anaphylaxis:

  • Don't give anything by mouth if person is having trouble breathing
  • Don't place person upright if they're feeling faint
  • Don't leave person alone
  • Don't assume one dose of epinephrine will be enough
  • Don't drive yourself to hospital if having reaction

Prevention Tips:

  • Know your allergens and avoid them
  • Read food labels carefully
  • Wear medical alert bracelet or necklace
  • Inform restaurants about food allergies
  • Carry emergency medications at all times
  • Have action plan written by doctor
  • Teach family and friends how to help
